**Writing Storybooks for Children Course**

**EXTRA! Brainstorming Ideas for Children’s Storybooks**

Perhaps you’re stuck for ideas, but have a passion for writing for children. Most writers hit a wall now and again, but the five techniques below should help ease you out of any block, so give them a go!

**Think back to your own childhood.** Divide one page of a journal or a scrap of paper into the following columns:

Happiest Memories

Saddest Memories

Most Embarrassing Memories

Most Frustrating Memories

Funniest Memories

Without thinking too much, fill in each column. Don’t worry about perfect punctuation or spelling, scribble down the memories as fast as you can to ensure that they’re pure and raw. When you’ve finished, step away from the book and leave it for a few hours. When you look at it again, which memory calls out to you most? Expand on it. Add more detail – as much as you can recall. Is there something you can use here as the basis for a book?

**Look to the news or historical records as a source** for inspirational or emotional stories about children or even animals. Are there any heroes who could inspire a fictional character in your book? Alternatively, is there a historical period that fascinates you? How would children have lived in this period? What adversity would they have faced? Children’s author Geraldine McCaughrean has based many of her books on historical events, including ‘**The Kite Rider’*** *and ‘**Where the World Ends’**. Start researching and find out if there’s a nugget of an idea in the past.

**What do kids love?** Don’t just rely on your own memories for this. Sure, some things are universal and ageless, such as playing with toys, but times have moved on and play patterns and toy preferences have changed. Observe as many children as you can in as many age groups as you can. Take note of what makes them tick, roar with laughter, yell with indignation or feel challenged. Take your time over this. It’s hard to write for kids if you don’t truly know kids – and your notes will be a powerful source of ideas. Don’t forget to jot down dialogue too – you’ll be thankful for it later.

**What’s your area of expertise or your true passion?** Could this inspire the subject of your book? J.K. Rowling worked as a teacher before writing a book set in wizard school. Her teaching experience gave her insider knowledge when coming up with ideas about settings and characters. In the UK, former jockey and horse racing commentator Clare Balding writes children’s books about horses. In the US, baseball star Derek Jeter has written picture books and a middle-grade series about his favourite subject – baseball.

**Is there an existing story or fictional character that’s ripe for a refresh?** It’s important that the story or character is in the public domain and copyright free. Your best starting points are classic myths (think Greek, Roman, Norse, Persian and indigenous cultures), ancient legends, folktales and fairy tales. Can you send St George on an even more exciting dragon adventure or give Rapunzel’s hair special powers? What happens if Thor meets the Minotaur? What happens if Cupid lives now? Look to the past and existing stories to spark new ideas of your own. The possibilities are endless.
